China's Cultural Revolution

1969-1971: Lin Biao

In 1969, General Lin Biao was officially declared Mao's successor. Between 1966 and 1968, China was isolated internationally, having declared its enmity towards both the Soviet Union and the United States. The friction with the Soviet Union intensified after border clashes on the Ussuri River. Amidst the tension, Lin Biao issued an executive order directing the army to prepare for war. Mao saw this as a power grab by his successor and began to maneuver against him. In 1971, Lin died in an airplane crash in Mongolia, apparently in an attempt to flee China and seek asylum in the Soviet Union. High-ranking members of Lin's military were subsequently purged, and Zhou Enlai, an ally of Mao, took over control of the government. Lin's death led many Chinese citizens to feel disillusioned over the course of Mao's revolution, which seemed to have dissolved in favor of ordinary power struggles.
